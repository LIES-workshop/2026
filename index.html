LIES 2026

Language Models, the Information Environment, and Society

A full-day workshop on language models and their societal consequences.

About

Large language models are now embedded infrastructure in how millions of people seek, produce, and engage with information—integrated into search engines, social media feeds, and news platforms, and increasingly used as conversational interfaces for navigating complex political and social topics. Their societal consequences are measurable and significant: LLMs embed political biases that shift users' opinions, moderation practices suppress certain voices, and AI-mediated information-seeking may erode depth of understanding.

Yet research on these effects remains fragmented: NLP and machine learning communities focus on system development, while communication, political science, and social science communities study downstream effects—and few researchers bridge both. This workshop uses the information environment as an integrating frame to connect work on political bias, content moderation, epistemic effects, and civic discourse, fostering cross-disciplinary exchange between LLM developers and researchers studying real-world societal impact.

Themes & Topics

The workshop invites contributions on, but not limited to, the following themes:

  • Political and ideological bias in LLMs Measurement, sources, and downstream effects on users and democratic processes.
  • LLMs and civic discourse Persuasion, deliberation, polarization, and democratic participation.
  • Misinformation and fact-checking LLM capabilities, limitations, and deployment for information verification.
  • Content moderation Differential treatment of topics and identities, censorship, and platform governance.
  • Information-seeking and epistemic autonomy Learning, over-reliance, and the right to reach one's own conclusions.
  • Evaluation and benchmarks Datasets, metrics, and frameworks for assessing socially consequential LLM behavior.
  • Interventions and governance Technical, policy, and design approaches to mitigating harms from LLMs in the information environment.

Submissions

We welcome two types of submissions, both of which will undergo double-blind peer review. Submissions should be made via OpenReview.

  • Archival papers 4–8 pages (excluding references). Full research papers included in the workshop proceedings.
  • Non-archival extended abstracts 2–4 pages (excluding references). Suitable for work in progress, preliminary findings, or work published or under review elsewhere.
